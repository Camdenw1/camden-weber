Running is weird. Most people hate it. Yet millions of people voluntarily wake up early, lace up their shoes, and go run miles for fun. Somehow, the thing that feels miserable at first becomes something runners genuinely look forward to.

Everyone can be a runner. When I say "runner," I define it as someone who consistently runs week to week — even when they are not training for something — and they enjoy the activity.

Many people claim they are not runners and can never become runners. They don't even see it as a possibility. I see it differently. I confidently believe everyone can be a runner. It just may take a journey to get there. So here's mine.

**Quick stats:** 3:27 marathon. 1:39 half marathon. Years of lacrosse, basketball, and even more sports during my youth.

---

## Pre-College Days

I would like to pinpoint the start of my journey at youth team sports. These are invaluable experiences where you learn how to be an athlete, work with others, be coachable, and work towards a goal. It was here where I spent so much time as a kid running around during practice and games. I played tee ball, soccer, football, basketball, and my main sport — lacrosse.

There were also times when I was younger where I ran races with my parents, which most definitely has had an influence on my running even to this day. This year, I am running the OG San Diego Triple Crown, which my parents did years ago. Through elementary school, they had one day a week where you would run laps during recess. They had creative tokens you earned including special milestone ones. I didn't realize it at the time, but those young runs were great foreshadowing of what I would end up doing later in life.

Lacrosse coupled with casual basketball took over my athletic life from ages 11 to 18. Through lacrosse, I learned not only how to build legs to handle the demands of a full season, but also how to sprint for extended stretches while staying agile. The sport itself forced my body to adapt and learn how to run quicker — because often the motivation to run quicker meant scoring goals and avoiding hits.

> Many people don't even know how to sprint because in the modern world there are rarely occasions that call for it. But if you can sprint comfortably, you can definitely run comfortably.

---

## College Days

In college, I started getting the urge to run to places. A personal challenge of sorts. I would wonder how long it would take to run to the beach, and then I would just go and do it. These have been some of the best experiences yet. I've explored so many places just by following curiosity runs. It turned running into a trip feeling instead of a workout feeling.

My personal philosophy on athletics emphasizes prioritizing the activities of movement and exercise that you find enjoyable, meaningful, and fun. What I love about these runs is the uncertainty — you don't know exactly what you'll see, how hard it will be, or what kind of experience you'll come back with.

A big milestone in my journey was getting a few guys from my fraternity to run with me to Santa Monica Pier from UCLA. This run ended up being the furthest I had ever run at the time (~6 miles) and also led to some great memories like going into the freezing water and riding public transit in LA for the first time.

### The Marathon Decision

Starting my junior year of college, I set my sights on running a marathon — something I had always known I wanted to do at some point in my life. This was the first time I started to actually research running: optimal training, form, nutrition, injury prevention, shoes, and run types. I became fluent in these areas, which I expected. What I didn't expect was the amount of exploring that running long distances allows.

During training, I explored many, many miles of LA by foot. Some parts I ran many times, others just once. This made me feel so much more connected to the city I had lived in for four years. Additionally, when I was traveling, I started using running as a way to explore new cities. It feels like every run is a collectable — and I am starting to take pride in the collection of places I've run.

I finished the Napa Valley Marathon with a time of **3:27** — a time I am very proud of. This was the hardest thing I have done in my life. While it was a great marker of physical accomplishment, it also felt like a signal of discipline, mental strength, and the overall ability to attack big, daunting tasks.

## Post-College Days

The time after a marathon is a big fork in the road for most people. Some are content and stop running. The other group continues because running gave them something positive. Running calms the mind, builds muscle, strengthens the heart, brings you on adventures, acts as a social event, and overall positively impacts people's lives. These benefits have done great things for me and I believe it is the reason I stuck with it.

So far this year, I completed a half marathon with a **1:39 finish** — about a 7:33 min/mile pace. I did this with only 10 training runs, mainly enjoyable long runs. I was in rough shape before training started and you might wonder how that's possible.

A few reasons:

- I can still feel the residual benefits from the marathon nearly two years ago
- I am a far more experienced runner now — with a full marathon under my belt, I feel much more in tune with my body
- Most importantly, I dedicated my life to staying active — hiking, tennis, basketball, cross training — which made the training feel like a natural extension of how I already live, not a chore

### What's Next

I want to keep this going for my whole life, with phases of harder training sprinkled in to hit specific goals. My most notable current goal is a **sub-1:30 half marathon**. A 1:39 was extremely hard for me. My next half will be a harder course and I want to hit 1:35 — but I know that will be a real challenge. I'm going for it anyway.

---

## Closing Thoughts

I can confidently say that I am a runner now. I've learned so much over this journey. It is one of the few things in life I know will boost my mood for the day, and I am grateful for that.

I hope more and more people join the running community — it is one of the most positive spaces out there. I will continue to write more about my running experience: curiosity runs, race experiences, and what I've learned along the way.

Running at the end of the day is a combination of movement and endurance, similar to what's found in the best days in life. It allows you to be up and at 'em for hours on end. Your days rarely end because you are tired. Running gives you the ability to experience more life.
