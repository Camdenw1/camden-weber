Last month I hiked a trail I'd never done before with a map I trusted too much. Somewhere around mile eight, the trail split in a way the map didn't show. No cell signal. Two options in front of me, neither one labeled.

I stood there longer than I should have, waiting for certainty that wasn't coming.

## The wrong instinct

My first instinct was to wait. Maybe there was more information I was missing. Maybe if I looked at the map hard enough, a detail would emerge that would tell me exactly which way to go.

That's not how it works on trails. And it's not how it works with data either.

## Confidence vs. certainty

There's a difference between being confident and being certain. Certainty is rare — in the woods and in data science. A model doesn't know the truth. It makes a best guess based on what it's seen.

What I've learned is that the goal isn't to eliminate uncertainty. It's to be honest about how much of it there is, make the best call you can with available information, and keep moving.

I took the left fork. It was the right call. Not because I knew — I didn't — but because the terrain, the sun angle, and the way the vegetation thinned suggested it was more traveled.

## What this has to do with anything

Uncertainty quantification is one of the most underrated skills in applied ML. It's not enough to produce a prediction. You need to know how confident to be in it, and you need to communicate that clearly.

The trail split forced me to think probabilistically. I didn't need 100% confidence. I needed 51%.

---

I made it back before dark. The map was just wrong.
