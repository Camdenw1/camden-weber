There's a particular feeling you get when you're standing somewhere unfamiliar, slightly tired, not entirely sure how far you are from home. It feels like travel. It feels like adventure. Most people assume you need a plane ticket to get there.

In reality, you don't. You just need to start running toward something you're curious about. Something that gets feeling a sense of wonder.

## The Concept

A curiosity run is simple: you pick a destination that seems interesting or ambitious, and you run to it. Not a mapped route. Not a predetermined distance. A target. A point on the horizon that makes you wonder what it looks like up close, how long it takes to get there, and what you'll find along the way.

The mountain you've driven past a hundred times. The cliff at the end of the coastal road. The neighborhood you've never had a reason to walk through. The beach that's always just been "about nine miles away."

You commit, you go, and you figure the rest out in motion.

## Where This Started

I've been doing these runs since I was a kid without ever having a name for them. Growing up in San Diego, I knew the beach was roughly nine miles from my house. One day I decided to find out what that actually meant on foot. The route took me through streets in my own community I had never set foot on despite having lived there my whole life. I run those paths regularly now.

There's a mountain to the east of my house that always sat in the background of my daily life. At some point I started wondering how long it would take to get to the top from my front door. The answer turned out to be an hour. I picked one of the clearest days of the year without realizing it. Standing at the top, I could see multiple mountain ranges, Catalina Island, and the coast of Mexico simultaneously. That run cost me nothing and I still think about it to this day.

## What One Actually Looks Like

A recent curiosity run started at the northwest corner of Mission Bay with a simple goal of getting to the bottom of Point Loma.

The first half took me past Sea World and along the water, then south down Sunset Cliffs Boulevard into one of San Diego's most underrated stretches of coastline. Sunset Cliffs on foot was beautiful and an amazing way to spend my Saturday. I stopped at a local spot mid-run for an acai bowl and coffee, which is an excellent fueling strategy (a great excuse for a break).

At some point I did the math and realized Point Loma was farther than I'd estimated. The full out-and-back would have pushed me well past what my body had signed up for that morning. So I turned around at PLNU, logged sixteen miles, and went home satisfied. The original goal went unfinished yet this run was one of the better ones I've had. A run I will always remember.

That's the thing about curiosity runs, the destination is almost beside the point.

## Why These Feel Different

Most runs have a predetermined shape. You know the distance before you start. You know the route. You know roughly how you'll feel at mile four because you've been there before. That predictability is useful for training, but it removes something.

Curiosity runs reintroduce the unknown. You're problem-solving in real time: recalculating distance, making navigation decisions, encountering things you didn't expect. Roads you've driven a hundred times feel completely different on foot. The scale changes. Details surface. You notice things. You are so enthralled with all of these factors that you forget you are even running which is the beauty of it.

The psychological effect is genuinely similar to travel. You get the exploration and the mild disorientation and the satisfaction of having figured something out — all within a few miles of your house. It's one of the more efficient ways I've found to feel like you've actually done something with a morning.

## How to Do Your First One

It is very simple. Pick something you're actually curious about. Something you've looked at and wondered about. The further it feels from your normal routes the better.

Don't over-plan. A rough sense of direction and distance is enough. The point is to commit without having all the answers.

Bring your charged phone, water, and more fuel than you think you need. Curiosity runs have a way of going longer than expected so be ready with the essentials.

Listen to your body. The goal is the experience, not the finish line. Turning around early isn't failure, it's good judgment. Some of my best runs ended before I reached the original destination.

## The Big Idea

The easiest way to manufacture a journey is to be confident enough to commit to something hard and unknown. You don't need a trail system or a race entry or a training plan. You just need a direction and enough curiosity to find out what's there.

Most of the best runs I've ever had started with nothing more than that.
